# One Ocean Expedition

“The students kept saying and keep saying that it was a life changing, one in a lifetime experience. Lot of it has to do with that they get this feeling that they have accomplished something they never thought they would have accomplished. It was a combination of academic and sailing experience for the students, and they made a lot of friendships that will last for a long time.” - said Katja Susanna Enberg, Professor of fisheries science at the Department of Biological Sciences, Bergen University.

Enberg led an interdisciplinary [course on sustainability](https://www.uib.no/en/oneocean/140406/study-sustainability-pacific-ocean) for university students aboard the sailing vessel Statsraad Lehmkuhl between May and August of this year, while the ship was sailing in the Pacific-ocean. Enberg talked to Qubit extensively about her research background, the One Ocean Expedition, and her experience teaching about sustainability in the Pacific.

"The goal [of the One Ocean Expedition] is to raise awareness about sustainability and particularly ocean sustainability, and what's the role of the ocean in a sustainable future." - said Enberg. As the researcher explained, she got involved in the expedition, which is organized by the Statsraad Lehmkuhls Foundation, due to the University of Bergen renting the ship out for a period of four months in 2022, and deciding that there will be a course held on board.

Enberg has been studying "for quite a while the evolutionary effects of fishing, so how fishing affects, for example, life history traits of fish populations", while she has also been "working quite a lot on the practical fisheries management side as well, like quota setting, population assessments, and trying to count how many fish there are in the sea." Lately she said, she has been "trying to shift the focus even more to planetary sustainability, not just thinking about the sustainability of the fish stock, but also trying to think about the climate impact and the broader perspective as well."

Currently Enberg is taking part in a large interdisciplinary project, where as she said, they try to look into how both the biodiversity impact and carbon footprint of fishing could be reduced, while at the same time producing more food from the ocean. As she explained, besides working together on these issues with people from NGOs, the researchers are doing different kinds of statistical analysises and modelling exercises, and also have psychologists involved in the team, who are trying to understand what kind of seafood people want to eat.

Because of this background, and her experience in teaching students for up to two weeks on research vessels in the North Sea, Enberg said that she was asked to lead the course. She explained, that "it is quite obvious that you need to put students from different disciplines together" because that way they can "get an understanding that a lot of people actually want to work with the same goal, but they might be thinking completely differently." That's why she said that from the very beginning she wanted the course to be interdisciplinary, where students from a lot of different backgrounds are put together, with good representation from international students, not just Norwegians. In her experience, that "also enriches the learning quite a bit, when you get perspectives from someone else who is not exactly like you, and hasn't had exactly the same life."

Enberg said that when organizing the course, they also wanted to have different kind of teachers apart from her own field of biology, so teachers from climate science, psychology, law, and social anthropology. The researcher said that this way "the students would get a wider understanding of sustainability", which doesn't always match the students expectations of someone telling them how to live sustainably. Enberg said that she wanted "them to learn something bigger, than whether they should recycle or not. So trying to open up their thoughts and minds, and also open up to see that it's not so simple, and that's part of the problem, that you have to dare to kind of confront the complexity of sustainability."


There were some challenges during the trip. As Enberg explained, "One thing that obviously affected us a lot was COVID. If there hadn't been COVID, we would have had much more interaction with local societies, local researchers, and probably with school classes. On these islands, many of them hadn't had so much COVID, some of them just didn't want to have us at all there. If we were allowed, it was really not encouraged to have any social [interactions], that was a bit sad because when you sail to the Pacific it would be nice to interact with the people a bit more."
